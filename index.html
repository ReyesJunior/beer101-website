Welcome To Beer 101

Beer is one of the most popular beverages in the world. There are several different processes and many different components that many beer what it is. How is it made? Where does it come from? What kind of beer am I drinking right now? Talking to a beer enthusiasts, with very limited knowledge of the subject matter, can be tricky and a little daunting (understandably so). This site is dedicated to providing some beer-must-know's to those who wish to learn more about the world of beer.

What is Beer?

Let's start with the basics! Beer is an alcoholic beverage that is made from the fermentation of barley, hops, yeast, and water. Barley is a type of grain that is rich in starch and the main ingredient for making beer, it's analogous to grapes being the main ingredient for wine.

To make beer, you first must brew it. To brew it means to ferment it. The beer brewing process requires that you ferment a carbohyadate source (mainly barley, but it can technically come from any carbohydrate) and extract it's sugar supply. Once the sugar is free, yeast is added to the mix to begin fermentation. The fermentation process produces alcohol and carbon dioxide. Once the fermentation is completed, you have created beer!

Popular to contrary belief, beer "is not made from" hops. Hops contain essential oils and resins (sticky and flammable organic substances) and are used as an ingredient to add some "spice" to the beer's flavor. Hops are known for their distinctive bitterness and "hoppy" flavor.

Useful Beer Descriptors

Since there exists such a diverse and varied beer selection to choose from, it can be difficult to accurately describe all of them. Luckily, there are some universal descriptors that will help you talk about beer with others. Here are some of the terms that are used to describe the "taste" of beer:

  • Aggressive: Boldy assertive aroma and/or taste
  • Balanced: Equal representation of malt sweetness and hop bitterness in the flavor
  • Complex: Many flavors and sensations on the palate
  • Crisp: Highly carbonated
  • Diacetyl: Buttery or butterscotch-like flavor or aroma
  • Estery: Fruity aromas
  • Floral: Aromas that are reminiscent of flowers
  • Fruity: Flavors that are reminiscent of fruits
  • Hoppy: Earthy, spicy, or citric aromas and flavors of hops
  • Malty: Grainy, caramel-like
  • Roasty/Toasty: Malt (roasted grain) flavors
  • Robust: Rich and full-bodied

The following are two other terms commonly used to describe a beer, but they don't describe taste:

  • MouthFeel: is the tactile sensory experience of the whole insode of the mouth and throat - includes a sense of body.
  • Body: the sensation of fullness, or viscosity, of a beer on the palate, ranging from watery to creamy; generally described as thin- , light- , medium- , or full-bodied.

Tasting and Evalutating

When it comes to tasting and evaluating a beer, it is important to keep a few things in mind: Beer tasting is an art and it should require some knowledge of how beer brewing in order to fully apppreciate the beer tasting experience.

  • Inspect: Raise the beer in front of you and describe it's color and consistency.
  • Agitate: Swirl your beer in your glass so that the aromas (and little nuances) are stimulated and released. This will prepare the beer for the next steps.
  • Smell: Breathe in to savor the aromas. Agitate if need be.
  • Taste: Sip the beer, but resist the urge to swallow the beer. Experience the mmouthfeel and consistency of the body.
