A brief history of beer

Take a look back into the past and see the evolution of beer over the centuries!

Beer 101 Timeline

What has happened to beer since the first encounter between itself and a human being? How has beer and beer brewing evolved over the centuries? Here's a quick look at the 10 most important snapshots of influential moments in beer history! Select the timeline headers to inspect and read more!

Swipe Left or Right to navigate timeline
  • 8,500 B.C.

    The domestication of barley marks the beginning of the "Neolithic revolution", the transition from hunting and gathering to agriculture and establishing settlements. The first beer was an "ale", a drink made malted barely fermented at warm tempertures. This was the preferred choice of drink during this time, until the introduction of wine, made possible my the Greeks.

  • 500 A.D.

    During the Middle ages, northern Europe endure what we now know as the "Dark Ages". Two significant factors caused Ale to be the prefered drink during the "Dark ages". The first was the collapse of the Roman empire. The collapse caused wine, the cultural drink at the time, to sharply decline. The second factor was an environmental one. Grapes could not grow well outside the Mediterranean climate. Due to the plague and overall lack of sanitation during this time, ale was much safter to drink than water.

  • 800 A.D.

    Without refrigeration, ale would spoil. In order to combat the bacteria that caused the ale to spoil, hops was introduced. Slowly, it became more accepted as a preservative (and as a flavoring too). As it moved through Europe, ale made with hops became known as "bier".

  • 1300 A.D.

    Lagering, the act of storing beer in cold locations, is implemented in Bravia. Lacking ice or cooling machines, "Lagering" was done in caves. The cold temperatures allowed for the beer to be preserved for long while giving it a conditioined, clearer look.

  • 1516 A.D.

    The Duke of Bravia, William IV, orders that a "purity law" be established to regulate and control the ingredients that go into making beer. The "law", called the "Reinheitsgebot", limits the amount of water, barley, and hops that go into the brewing of beer. As a sign of purity, this "law" signify's the large scale that beer consumption reached during the 15th century.

  • 1620 A.D.

    The pilgrims land on plymouth rock because their beer supplies were running low.

  • 1810 A.D.

    Munich establishes "Oktoberfest" as an official celebration.

  • 1876 A.D

    After studying and working on "Etudes Sur la biere" (a study of yeast and microorganisms in the brewing process), Louis Pasteur develops pasteurization to stabilize beers. (22yrs before it is applied to milk).

  • 1882 A.D.

    A german brewer, Thausting, observes that the photosensitive enzymes in the hops within light beers broke down into sulfur-dioxide. This breakdown of the hops into sulfur-dioxide gave the beer a "skunky" aroma and taste. Beers were then bottled into brown bottles (to better filter light) instead of clearer bottles.

  • 1991

    The U.S. is the world's largest beer producer, producing over 20% of the world's beer. Estimated per capita consumption ranked the U.S. in 13th place.
